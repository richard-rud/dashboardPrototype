What are the effects?

The use of natural resources for food production is for industrialized countries inefficient (Kytzia, Faist and Baccini 2004). This has a tremendous affect on the earth's ecosystem (Wirsenius 2003), affecting water use, land use, loss of biodiversity and the carbon footprint (FAO 2013). This includes influencing nitrogen cycles, phosphorus (P) cycles and energy cycles among others, causing acidification, eutrophication and greenhouse gas emissions (Wirsenius 2003). Expected growth in food production due to population growth in the coming years (Kytzia, Faist and Baccini 2004) reinforces environmental concerns (Foley et al. 2013) and amplifies the need for effective resource management.

The phosphorus issue

Phosphorus (P) is a vital nutrient for food production and the linearity of anthropogenic P processes makes the use of P unsustainable. Current practices go from extraction to consumption to waste without a reuse phase, eventually leading to P accumulation in land and aquatic systems. It is expected that current practices will lead to depletion, unless P is used in a more circular way (Schmid Neset, Bader, Scheidegger and Lohm, 2008; Cordell, Drangert and White, 2009). While P losses aggravates scarcity issues, if accumulated in the wrong environmental compartments, P also acts as a pollutant (Yuan, Shi, Wu, Zhang and Bi, 2011).

What are the current strategies?

In the European Union

Traditionaly as seen from the pyramid, the EU focus has been on using the waste hierarchy principles, where prevention of food waste is top priority. In Norway strategies however tend to favor recycling solutions (grey).

What dominates Norwegian policy?

The Norwegian government has acknowledged that food waste is a major issue, and that reduction of food waste is high on the political agenda (Brekk 2010). However, according to Hamilton (2015), it is not clear whether food waste prevention is the prevailing strategy. As opposed to what the government claims, it appears that strategies focus on recycling solutions for creating energy, rewarding the production of bioenergy from organic waste. This is reflected in the state budget as well as the new intersectoral biogas strategy (FIN 2015; Hamilton 2015; KLD 2014).

But there are not only negative effects...

Contributor to energy production

Food waste is also contributing to energy production. In Norway today a substantial amount of food waste is becoming input to biogas production, and the potential is much larger than what is utilized today.

Deconding the complexity

We do not aimlessly want to follow the waste hierarchy. We need a systems approach, in order to quantify mass, energy and critical material flows for food production. Food waste affects multiple actors as well as environmental resources (Hamilton 2015). Since we are dealing with a multi-dimensional problem, we have to consider system-wide effects. For this job, we use MFA (Material Flow Anlysis) for qunatifying the mass, energy and material flows of the Norwegian food production system.

So, should prevention strategies dominate policy?

It is not clear whether food waste prevention is the best strategy, since we dont know what the actual environmental benefits from food waste prevention and how these differ from food waste recycling. Therefore a systematic assessment of the mass, energy and critical material flows is crucial. This has never been carried out for the agriculture and waste sector in Norway (Hamilton 2015). Hamilton (2015) has researched the Norwegian food waste system, quantifying all the flows and stocks, showing the inputs, outputs, throughput and accumulation of biomass, energy and P. This study also includes feature scenarios, comparing food waste prevention versus food waste recycling. And is an important first step in quantifying the effects of food waste and the possible problem shifting that might occur between different policy decisions.
